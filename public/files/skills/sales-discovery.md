# Sales Discovery — Structured Discovery Call Framework

## When to use
Use when building discovery call scripts, preparing for sales conversations, or creating talk tracks for any situation where you need to understand a prospect's current setup, pain points, and decision criteria before pitching. Works for competitive displacement, new category creation, or standard B2B sales.

## Discovery Structure

### The 15-Second Opener

Every discovery call starts with context. The prospect should know within 15 seconds:
1. Who you are
2. Why you're talking to them specifically
3. What you're going to ask (permission to ask questions)

**Template:**
> "Hey [name], thanks for taking this. I'm [your name], [role] at [company]. You [context: downloaded our comparison / replied to my email / signed up for free credits]. Before I show you anything, I want to understand what you're working with now so I can make the next 20 minutes useful. Cool if I ask a few questions?"

**Rules:**
- Under 15 seconds spoken
- Reference how they got here (inbound trigger, outbound reply, referral)
- Ask permission before interrogating them
- No "tell me about your company" — you should already know

### Discovery Question Framework

Organize questions into 3 categories. Ask 2-3 from each based on the conversation flow.

**Category 1: Current State**
- What tool/provider are you using today for [problem area]?
- How long have you been using it?
- How many people on your team use it?
- What does the data flow look like? (Where does it come in, where does it go?)
- What's working well about your current setup?

**Category 2: Pain Points**
- What's the biggest gap in what you're getting back?
- When was the last time the data quality caused a problem downstream?
- What happens when a record comes back incomplete?
- If you could change one thing about your current provider, what would it be?
- Are there use cases you've wanted to build but couldn't because of data limitations?

**Category 3: Decision Criteria**
- If you were to evaluate a switch, what would you need to see?
- Who else would be involved in that decision?
- What's your timeline? (Contract renewal date if applicable)
- What does a successful test look like for your team?
- Is budget allocated, or would this need approval?

### Competitive Intel Questions

When the call is a competitive displacement conversation:
- "What made you choose [competitor] originally?"
- "Have you looked at alternatives recently, or is this the first time?"
- "What does [competitor] do well that you'd want us to match?"
- "What's the one thing that frustrates you most about [competitor]?"
- "If I could show you [specific advantage] on your own data, would that be worth 24 hours of testing?"

### Objection Handling Framework

For any objection, use this structure:

1. **Acknowledge** — Validate their concern. Never dismiss.
2. **Reframe** — Shift the frame to where you're strong.
3. **Proof** — Offer specific evidence (number, customer name, test offer).
4. **Bridge** — Connect back to next step.

**Common objections and responses:**

**"We're happy with [competitor]"**
> Acknowledge: "Good — means you already see the value of [category]."
> Reframe: "I'm not saying they're bad. I'm saying there's a measurable difference in [specific metric]."
> Proof: "[Specific number or customer example]."
> Bridge: "The only way to know if that matters for you is to test. Takes [timeframe]. No commitment."

**"We don't need [your differentiator]"**
> Acknowledge: "Totally fair — and that's not the reason to switch."
> Reframe: "The reason to switch is that we're better on [the thing they DO need]."
> Proof: "[Specific metric on the overlap area]."
> Bridge: "Run the test on [overlap area] alone. If we're not better there, fair enough."

**"We're locked into a contract"**
> Acknowledge: "When does it end?"
> Reframe: "You can run the test now and have your answer ready before renewal."
> Proof: "Most teams that switch tell us they wish they'd tested sooner."
> Bridge: "The test results don't expire. Run it now, decide later."

**"Too small / never heard of you"**
> Acknowledge: "Fair — we're not a household name yet."
> Reframe: "But the data speaks."
> Proof: "[Customer names, partnership names, specific metrics]."
> Bridge: "The test makes our company size irrelevant. You'll see the results on your own data."

**"No bandwidth to switch"**
> Acknowledge: "Integration concerns are real."
> Reframe: "Most integrations take [timeframe]. Same API pattern you have now."
> Proof: "[Customer] replaced their entire stack with one integration."
> Bridge: "We do white-glove setup. Our engineering team pairs with yours."

### Segment-Specific Discovery

Customize your questions based on the prospect's segment:

**Technical builders (engineers, data scientists)**
- Go deeper on API design, data schema, response format
- Ask about their current data pipeline architecture
- Talk about fill rates, accuracy, standardization methodology
- Demo the API response, not a dashboard

**GTM/Sales/Marketing**
- Focus on match rates, contact info quality, deliverability
- Ask about their current enrichment workflow and tools
- Talk about ROI: time saved, reply rates improved, pipeline generated
- Demo the enriched profile, not the API call

**Founders/Executives**
- Focus on pricing, competitive advantage, speed to value
- Ask about their product roadmap and where data fits
- Talk about customer proof points and partnerships
- Keep the conversation strategic, not technical

**Product leaders**
- Focus on what data enables in their product
- Ask what features they'd build with better data
- Talk about competitive differentiation through data depth
- Demo the "wow" fields that would excite their users

### Post-Discovery Next Steps

Always end with a clear, specific next step:
1. **If convinced:** "Send me [X records]. I'll have results in [timeframe]."
2. **If interested but cautious:** "I'll set you up with free [credits/trial]. No credit card."
3. **If needs internal buy-in:** "Let me send you [results + pricing]. Who else should I include?"
4. **If not ready:** "When does your [contract/quarter/project] end? I'll follow up then."

Never end a call with "I'll send you some info." Always specify what info, by when, and what you need from them.
